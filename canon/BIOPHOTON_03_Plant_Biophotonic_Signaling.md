## 1. What Plants Emit

All living plants emit ultraweak photon emission (UPE) continuously, with several documented properties:

- **Spectral range:** UV to near-infrared (200–950nm), with peaks in the blue-green (400–550nm) and near-red (600–700nm) bands
- **Intensity:** 10–1,000 photons/cm²/second — similar to animal tissue
- **Metabolic correlation:** Emission intensity rises during photosynthesis, germination, stress response, and injury
- **Spatial structure:** Emission is not uniform — different organs (roots, stems, leaves) emit at different intensities and with different spectral profiles

The emission pattern showed that the plant optimises its biophotonic output at the population level — individual nodes modulate their emission to increase or decrease the **global benefit** for the whole organism. ## 2. Internal Plant Communication

### The MAPPI System (2026)

In February 2026, researchers using the new MAPPI (MAcro Plant Projection Imaging) platform visualised, for the first time, real-time bi-directional signaling between leaves, stems, and roots simultaneously. The results overturned the assumption that plant signals travel only from injury site outward.

Key finding: signals travel **bi-directionally** — not just from leaves to roots but from roots to leaves, and back — in response to stress. The communication network is more complex than expected: roots know what is happening to leaves, and leaves respond to what is happening to roots.

The chemical carriers are calcium ions and glutamate (identical to the neurotransmitter used in human neural signaling). But the *routing* and *timing* of these signals is coordinated by the plant's electrical and photonic field — the chemical messengers travel within a photonically-coordinated framework.

### Wound Response Biophotonics

When a plant is injured — caterpillar bite, drought, salt stress, burning — UPE intensity spikes sharply and *specifically at the injury site*, then propagates outward in a wave pattern. This has been documented in *Arabidopsis*, *Spathiphyllum*, and adzuki seedlings.

The photonic wave precedes and accompanies the chemical signal wave. The light travels faster than the chemistry. The biophotonic emission is the plant's **first response** — its fastest available channel for systemic communication.

## 3. External Plant Communication

Plants also emit biophotons *externally* — and there is growing evidence that neighbouring plants read each other's emission fields:

- **Stress contagion:** Plants adjacent to a UV-stressed neighbour show anticipatory stress responses before chemical volatile signals could have reached them — consistent with photonic signal reception
- **Bacterial synchronisation:** *E. coli* and other microorganisms show biophoton-mediated synchronisation between physically separated populations (glass vs. quartz experiments)
- **Soil mycorrhizal network coupling:** The fungal networks connecting plant root systems (the "wood wide web") may carry both chemical and photonic signals — the fungal hyphae have waveguide geometry consistent with optical signal conduction

## 5. Implications for the Century-Old Mystery (Solved: 2025)

In April 2025, Cornell University researchers published in *PNAS* the solution to a century-old question in plant biology: how do plants signal stress systemically so fast? The answer is **pressure-wave-coupled chemical transport**: a stressor causes a pressure change in the vasculature, which drives mass flow of chemical messengers (jasmonate, glutamate) far faster than diffusion alone would allow.

This is the mechanical-chemical layer of plant communication. The biophotonic layer is the *field layer* that runs beneath it — giving each cell positional and state information before the chemical wave arrives. The two systems are complementary, not competing.
