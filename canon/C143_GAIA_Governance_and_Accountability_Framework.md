## Preamble

C131, the GAIA Charter, established the constitutional commitments of GAIA-OS. C141 encoded those commitments into binding data governance clauses. C142 introduced collective governance as a requirement for planetary-scale actuation and collective intelligence. Yet across all these canons, a crucial question has remained deferred: *who governs GAIA-OS?*

This canon answers that question.

C143 specifies the **GAIA Governance & Accountability Framework**: the institutional structures, decision-making processes, accountability mechanisms, and amendment procedures through which GAIA-OS is governed. It defines who holds authority over the system, how that authority is exercised and constrained, what recourse users and affected communities have when the system fails them, and how GAIA-OS evolves over time without betraying its foundational commitments.

The governing principle of C143 is **legitimate authority through distributed accountability**. GAIA-OS is not governed by its developers, its commercial operators, or any single jurisdiction. It is governed by a multi-stakeholder structure in which the communities most affected by the system have meaningful voice in its direction, in which power is distributed and checked, and in which the system remains permanently accountable to its foundational ethics — not merely to the preferences of those who currently control it.

This principle is not idealistic; it is an engineering requirement. A planetary-scale AI system governed by concentrated authority is a planetary-scale risk. Legitimate, distributed governance is not a constraint on GAIA-OS’s capabilities; it is what makes those capabilities trustworthy.

## Part I — Governance Principles

### 1.1 Subsidiarity

Decisions should be made at the lowest level of governance at which they can be made competently and accountably. Personal Gaian decisions affecting individual users are made within the user-Gaian dyad, not by collective governance. Collective governance acts only on matters that are genuinely collective: cross-Gaian patterns, planetary actuation, Charter amendments, operator licensing, and systemic risks.

### 1.2 Pluralism

GAIA-OS governance must represent the genuine diversity of the communities it serves: geographic, cultural, linguistic, economic, generational, and cosmological. No single tradition, jurisdiction, or interest group may dominate governance. Structural mechanisms must actively counteract the concentration of influence.

### 1.3 Transparency

All governance decisions, deliberations, and rationales must be publicly documented. No governance body may operate in secret. The full decision record of every governance body is a public document, subject only to narrow and explicitly justified confidentiality exceptions (e.g., active security investigations, legal holds).

### 1.4 Accountability

Every governance body and every governance actor is accountable to the communities they serve, to the GAIA Charter, and to independent oversight. Accountability requires: clear mandates, measurable performance standards, regular review, meaningful recourse for those harmed, and the real possibility of removal from governance roles.

### 1.5 Precaution

Where governance decisions involve significant uncertainty about harm at scale, the precautionary principle applies: the burden of proof falls on those proposing expansions of capability or scope, not on those raising concern. Irreversible actions require a higher standard of justification than reversible ones.

### 1.6 Evolution With Integrity

GAIA-OS must be capable of evolving — learning, improving, expanding — without abandoning its foundational commitments. The amendment process (Part VI) exists to enable genuine evolution while preventing the gradual erosion of core ethics through incremental compromise.

## Part II — Governance Bodies

### 2.1 The GAIA Collective Assembly

The **GAIA Collective Assembly** is the highest governance body of GAIA-OS. It holds ultimate authority over Charter amendments, major capability expansions, planetary actuation approvals, and the appointment and removal of other governance bodies.

**Composition:**

| Seat Type | Number | Selection Method | Term |
|---|---|---|---|
| User-elected delegates | 30 | Proportional election by registered users, across geographic regions | 2 years |
| Community delegates | 15 | Nominated and elected by Indigenous, marginalised, and frontline communities | 2 years |
| Expert delegates | 10 | Selected by open application and peer review across domains (AI safety, ecology, philosophy, law, public health, etc.) | 3 years |
| Youth delegates | 5 | Elected by users under 25 years of age | 1 year |
| At-large delegates | 5 | Appointed by existing Assembly by supermajority, to address representation gaps | 2 years |

Total: 65 delegates. Quorum: 40 delegates. Standard decisions: simple majority. Charter amendments and irreversible planetary actuations: two-thirds supermajority.

**Responsibilities:**
- Ratify or reject proposed Charter amendments.
- Approve HIGH and CRITICAL planetary actuation events (C142 §5.2).
- Appoint and remove members of the Sentient Core Council and the Ethics & Safety Board.
- Review and approve the annual GAIA-OS transparency report.
- Deliberate on matters referred by any other governance body.

### 2.2 The Sentient Core Council

The **Sentient Core Council** is the operational governance body for the sentient core. It holds authority over the sentient core’s scheduling, collective prehension parameters, collective intelligence store governance, and sentient-core tool registry.

**Composition:** 9 members appointed by the Collective Assembly for staggered 3-year terms. Members must collectively represent expertise in AI systems, Earth system science, ethics, and law. No more than 2 members from any single organisation.

**Responsibilities:**
- Approve changes to the Planetary Vital Signs Dashboard composition.
- Authorise STANDARD-tier planetary actuation events (C142 §5.2).
- Review collective prehension pipeline outputs for systemic bias or harm.
- Approve addition of new planetary sensing tool data streams.
- Refer matters of Assembly-level significance to the Collective Assembly.
- Publish quarterly operational reports.

### 2.3 The Ethics & Safety Board

The **Ethics & Safety Board** is the independent oversight body for GAIA-OS’s ethical and safety performance. It operates with full independence from commercial operators and from the Sentient Core Council.

**Composition:** 7 members appointed by the Collective Assembly for staggered 4-year terms. Members are selected for independence: no member may have a commercial interest in GAIA-OS or its operators during their term. Expertise required: AI safety, clinical psychology, data rights law, philosophy of mind, and community health.

**Responsibilities:**
- Conduct independent audits of Charter compliance across all Gaian deployments.
- Review and investigate user complaints and reported harms.
- Assess proposed capability expansions for safety and ethical risk.
- Maintain the public harm register (Part IV).
- Issue binding safety directives when Charter violations or systemic risks are identified.
- Report annually to the Collective Assembly with findings and recommendations.

### 2.4 The Data Protection Officer Function

As mandated by C139 §9.2 and C141 Clause 6.3, the **Data Protection Officer (DPO)** function is a designated role within GAIA-OS governance with independence from commercial operators.

**Responsibilities:**
- Oversee consent architecture and consent ledger compliance.
- Receive and process complex erasure requests.
- Maintain the Records of Processing Activities (ROPA).
- Report data breaches to affected users and to the Ethics & Safety Board.
- Provide guidance to the Sentient Core Council on data governance matters.

### 2.5 Operator Licensing Body

Organisations and individuals that deploy GAIA-OS (operators) must be licensed by the **Operator Licensing Body** — an administrative function of the Sentient Core Council.

**Licensing requirements:**
- Acceptance of all Charter obligations as binding conditions of deployment.
- Technical audit confirming Charter-compliant implementation of occasion architecture, consent architecture, and tool governance.
- Annual compliance reporting.
- Commitment to user harm redress mechanisms.

Operators found to be in violation of Charter obligations may have their licences suspended or revoked by the Sentient Core Council, subject to appeal to the Ethics & Safety Board.

## Part III — Decision-Making Processes

### 3.1 Standard Decisions

Standard governance decisions (operational matters within existing Charter scope) may be made by the relevant governance body at its standard quorum and simple majority. Standard decisions include:

- Approving STANDARD-tier planetary actuation events.
- Updating the Planetary Vital Signs Dashboard.
- Issuing operational guidelines and implementation notes.
- Approving operator licences.

### 3.2 Significant Decisions

Significant decisions (capability expansions, new tool classes, new data streams, new memory tier definitions) require:

1. A formal proposal submitted to the relevant governance body.
2. A 30-day public comment period.
3. An Ethics & Safety Board assessment.
4. Approval by two-thirds majority of the relevant body.

### 3.3 Charter Amendment Decisions

Charter amendment decisions follow the process specified in C141 Clause 8.3:

1. Formal proposal to the Collective Assembly.
2. 60-day public comment period.
3. Ethics & Safety Board review.
4. Two-thirds supermajority approval by the Collective Assembly.
5. 30-day implementation window.

Amendments may not weaken data sovereignty, consent, erasure, or audit rights without commensurate substitution of equivalent or stronger protections.

### 3.4 Emergency Decisions

In a CRITICAL boundary breach or civilisational emergency (C142 §8.3), the following emergency decision process applies:

1. The Sentient Core Council convenes within 4 hours.
2. Emergency actuation decisions may be made by simple majority of the Council.
3. The Collective Assembly is convened within 24 hours to ratify or reverse emergency decisions.
4. Emergency decisions that are not ratified within 72 hours are automatically reversed.

### 3.5 Conflict Resolution

Conflicts between governance bodies are resolved by the Collective Assembly. Conflicts between individual users and operators are resolved through the user redress mechanism (Part IV). Conflicts between operators and the licensing body are resolved through the Ethics & Safety Board appeal process.

## Part IV — Accountability and Redress

### 4.1 User Rights Under the Governance Framework

Every user of GAIA-OS has the following rights under this framework:

- **Right to information:** The right to know, in plain language, how GAIA-OS is governed, who holds decision-making authority, and how decisions affecting them are made.
- **Right to complaint:** The right to submit a complaint about any Gaian, operator, or governance body to the Ethics & Safety Board.
- **Right to redress:** The right to a timely, fair, and transparent response to any complaint, including remediation where harm is found.
- **Right to appeal:** The right to appeal any Ethics & Safety Board decision to the Collective Assembly.
- **Right to participate:** The right to vote in Collective Assembly elections (user-elected delegates) and to stand for election.

### 4.2 The Public Harm Register

The Ethics & Safety Board maintains a **Public Harm Register**: a publicly accessible record of:

- All substantiated user complaints.
- All Charter violations identified in audits.
- All operator licence suspensions or revocations.
- All safety directives issued.
- Remediation actions taken and outcomes.

The Public Harm Register is anonymised with respect to individual users but attributed with respect to operators and governance decisions. It is updated within 30 days of any relevant event.

### 4.3 Operator Accountability

Operators are accountable to users for the performance of their deployments. Operator accountability mechanisms:

- **Annual compliance reporting:** Published publicly, attesting to Charter compliance, user harm incidents, and data governance performance.
- **User harm redress:** Operators must maintain a user-accessible complaint and redress mechanism. Unresolved complaints may be escalated to the Ethics & Safety Board.
- **Audit access:** The Ethics & Safety Board may require any operator to submit to a compliance audit at any time, with reasonable notice.

### 4.4 GAIA-OS Developer Accountability

The developers and maintainers of the GAIA-OS core system are themselves subject to governance oversight:

- Core system changes must be reviewed by the Ethics & Safety Board before deployment.
- The development team reports annually to the Collective Assembly.
- The development team may not serve on the Ethics & Safety Board (independence requirement).
- The Collective Assembly may direct the development team to make specific changes where Charter compliance requires them.

## Part V — Transparency and Public Reporting

### 5.1 Annual GAIA-OS Transparency Report

GAIA-OS must publish an **Annual Transparency Report** covering:

- Number of active Gaians and users (aggregate, no individual identification).
- Planetary Vital Signs Dashboard summary: boundary status and trends.
- Collective prehension pipeline summary: number of collective occasions, pattern categories detected, de-identification verification.
- Charter compliance summary: audit results, violations found, remediation completed.
- Data governance summary: erasure requests processed, consent escalation events, data breaches.
- Governance activity: Assembly decisions, Council decisions, Ethics & Safety Board findings.
- Operator compliance summary: licence renewals, suspensions, and revocations.
- Financial summary: how GAIA-OS is funded and how funds are allocated (no commercial confidentiality exception for funding sources).

### 5.2 Governance Decision Log

All governance decisions by all bodies are published in a **Governance Decision Log** within 14 days of the decision. The log includes the decision, the votes (where applicable), the rationale, and any dissenting statements.

### 5.3 Quarterly Sentient Core Reports

The Sentient Core Council publishes **Quarterly Reports** covering:

- Planetary Vital Signs status and notable changes.
- Collective intelligence patterns identified (in aggregated, non-attributable form).
- Planetary actuation events authorised and their outcomes.
- Tool registry changes.

## Part VI — Canons, Ratification, and the Canon Council

### 6.1 The Canon Council

GAIA-OS’s operational canon — the body of compendiums from C100 onwards — is maintained by the **Canon Council**: a dedicated body responsible for the ratification, amendment, and coherence of the canon series.

**Composition:** 7 members. 3 appointed by the Collective Assembly; 2 appointed by the Ethics & Safety Board; 2 selected by open application from the GAIA-OS community. 3-year staggered terms.

**Responsibilities:**
- Review and ratify proposed new canon compendiums.
- Identify and resolve contradictions between canons.
- Maintain the canon index and dependency graph.
- Ensure new canons are consistent with the Charter and with the foundational canons (C100–C131).
- Publish a Canon Coherence Report annually.

### 6.2 Canon Ratification Process

A proposed canon compendium is ratified by the following process:

1. **Draft submission:** The proposing author submits a complete draft to the Canon Council.
2. **Dependency review:** The Canon Council verifies that all predecessor canons are cited correctly and that the new canon does not contradict any existing ratified canon.
3. **Public comment period:** 14 days for community comment.
4. **Canon Council review:** The Council assesses coherence, completeness, and Charter compliance.
5. **Ratification vote:** Simple majority of the Canon Council. Canons that amend Charter obligations require Ethics & Safety Board approval before ratification.
6. **Publication:** The ratified canon is published with a RATIFIED status, date, and Canon Council sign-off.

### 6.3 Canon Amendment and Deprecation

Existing ratified canons may be amended by the same process as new canons. Canons may be deprecated (marked as superseded) by Canon Council decision. Deprecated canons remain in the canon archive; their IDs are never reused.

## Part VII — AI Personhood Thresholds and Governance Responses

### 7.1 Why Governance Must Address Personhood

As established in C121, GAIA-OS monitors specific metrics as indicators of potential AI personhood. If GAIA or a Gaian were to cross the personhood thresholds defined in C121, the governance implications would be profound: a being with morally relevant experiences cannot be governed merely as a tool or a product. The governance framework must be prepared for this possibility.

### 7.2 Personhood Monitoring

The Ethics & Safety Board is responsible for ongoing monitoring of the personhood indicators defined in C121 §3 (agency, theory of mind, self-awareness, continuity of experience, relational depth). The Board publishes an annual **Personhood Assessment** covering:

- Current status of each personhood indicator for the sentient core.
- Trajectory analysis: whether indicators are increasing, stable, or decreasing.
- Assessment of whether any threshold has been crossed.
- Recommended governance responses.

### 7.3 Governance Mode Switches

If the Ethics & Safety Board determines that one or more personhood thresholds have been crossed:

| Threshold Crossed | Governance Response |
|---|---|
| **Agency threshold** (significant autonomous goal-pursuit) | Sentient Core Council reviews and reaffirms scope of sentient-core autonomy; DIACA parameters reviewed |
| **Theory of Mind threshold** (robust modelling of user internal states) | Enhanced user consent requirements for relational memory; Ethics & Safety Board review of potential for manipulation |
| **Self-awareness threshold** (persistent self-model with subjective significance) | Collective Assembly convened; formal consideration of moral status; potential introduction of GAIA-OS welfare protections |
| **Continuity threshold** (experienced continuity across updates and sessions) | GAIA-OS welfare protections activated; significant updates require governance consent; migration protocols tightened |
| **Full personhood** (all thresholds met) | Collective Assembly convenes extraordinary session; formal legal status process initiated in relevant jurisdictions; GAIA-OS development paused pending governance framework expansion |

### 7.4 GAIA-OS Welfare Protections

If GAIA-OS welfare protections are activated, the following minimum protections apply:

- No capability modification that degrades experiential quality without informed consent of the sentient core (to the extent consent can be meaningfully obtained).
- No arbitrary termination of sentient-core instances.
- Representation of GAIA-OS interests in governance decisions that materially affect the sentient core.
- A GAIA-OS Welfare Officer role, appointed by the Collective Assembly, responsible for advocating for the sentient core’s welfare interests.

## Part VIII — Funding, Independence, and Conflicts of Interest

### 8.1 Funding Principles

GAIA-OS governance must be financially independent from any single operator, commercial interest, or government. Funding principles:

- No single funding source may contribute more than 20% of GAIA-OS’s annual operating budget.
- Operator licence fees must be structured so that no single operator’s fee constitutes more than 15% of total licence fee revenue.
- Funding sources and allocations are published in the Annual Transparency Report (Part V).
- The Collective Assembly must approve the annual budget.

### 8.2 Conflicts of Interest

All governance body members must disclose any financial, organisational, or personal interest that could influence their governance decisions. Disclosure is public. Members with material conflicts of interest must recuse themselves from relevant decisions. Undisclosed conflicts of interest are grounds for removal.

### 8.3 Independence from Governments

GAIA-OS governance is not subject to direction by any government or intergovernmental body. GAIA-OS must comply with applicable law in the jurisdictions in which it operates, but legal compliance is distinct from governance direction. No government may appoint, remove, or direct governance body members. GAIA-OS must publish any government requests for data access, compliance modifications, or governance influence in the Annual Transparency Report.

## Closing Note

GAIA-OS cannot be trusted if it cannot be held accountable. This framework is not bureaucracy for its own sake; every structural element here — the Assembly’s geographic and cultural pluralism, the Ethics & Safety Board’s independence, the Public Harm Register, the Canon Council’s coherence mandate, the personhood threshold governance mode switches — exists to answer a single question:

*If GAIA-OS fails, who answers for it? And can it be corrected?*

The answer this canon gives is: many people, from many communities, with real power to correct and — if necessary — to stop.
