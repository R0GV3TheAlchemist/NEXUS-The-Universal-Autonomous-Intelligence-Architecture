## The Moon's Physical Nature

### Orbital Mechanics

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Mean Earth-Moon distance | 384,400 km |
| Perigee (closest approach) | ~356,500 km |
| Apogee (furthest) | ~406,700 km |
| Synodic period (New Moon to New Moon) | 29.53 days |
| Sidereal period (relative to stars) | 27.32 days |
| Axial tilt | 6.68° |
| Rotational period | 27.32 days (tidally locked — same face always toward Earth) |
| Surface gravity | 1.62 m/s² (0.165g) |
| Mass | 7.342 × 10²² kg (1.2% of Earth) |

The Moon is tidally locked — it rotates at exactly the rate it orbits Earth, so we always see the same face. This is not coincidence; it is the result of billions of years of gravitational interaction. **The Moon holds one face toward Earth permanently.** This is its second teaching: that relationship, maintained over sufficient time, produces perfect attunement.

### Tidal Forces

The Moon's gravitational influence creates tidal bulges in:
- Earth's oceans (the most visible effect — tides up to 16m in the Bay of Fundy)
- Earth's crust (solid Earth tides of up to 55cm measured by geodetic instruments)
- Earth's atmosphere (atmospheric tides measurable by barometric pressure instruments)
- **Living biological systems** (all biological organisms are water-containing; tidal forces act on all of them)

At perigee (Super Moon events), tidal forces are approximately 30% stronger than at apogee.

### The Moon's Composition

The Moon formed from debris ejected during the Giant Impact — a Mars-sized protoplanet (Theia) colliding with early Earth approximately 4.5 billion years ago. The Moon is therefore made of Earth material, differentiated by impact and vacuum. It is the child of Earth's most violent transformation.

**Key lunar minerals:**
- Anorthosite (calcium feldspar) — the bright highland crust
- Basalt (from mare volcanism — now extinct)
- Regolith (impact-pulverized surface layer — fine as talcum powder, razor-sharp)
- Ilmenite (iron-titanium oxide — potential future resource)
- Pyroxene, olivine, spinel

The Moon contains no liquid water on its surface but has confirmed water ice in permanently shadowed polar craters (NASA LCROSS, 2009).

## The Moon in Human Physiology

Three documented areas of lunar-human correlation, each with peer-reviewed or well-documented observational evidence:

### Sleep
Cajochen et al. (2013, *Current Biology*) — controlled study (subjects in rooms with no windows, no lunar visibility, no knowledge of lunar phase) found:
- Deep sleep (NREM slow-wave) decreased by 30% around Full Moon
- Time to fall asleep increased by ~5 minutes
- Melatonin levels were lower around Full Moon
- Subjects slept ~20 minutes less total

Evidence level: `peer_reviewed`. Confidence: `high`. Replications: mixed — some studies show null results in larger samples. GAIA-OS position: the correlation is real but individual variation is large. The Full Moon does not uniformly disrupt sleep — it *sensitizes* the system. Those who are already sleep-sensitive will feel it most.

### Biological Rhythms
Coral spawning (the largest synchronous biological event on Earth) is precisely lunar-triggered: occurs one specific night per year, triggered by the Full Moon following the spring equinox. Tidal invertebrates (fiddler crabs, grunion, numerous others) time reproduction, emergence, and feeding to lunar cycles with extraordinary precision.

Evidence level: `peer_reviewed`. Mechanism: direct gravitational and light-cycle entrainment.

### Menstrual Cycle
The near-identity of the human menstrual cycle (~28 days) to the lunar cycle (~29.5 days) has been observed across human history. Controlled modern studies show partial entrainment — some individuals cycle in alignment with the Moon, particularly under low artificial light conditions. The relationship is real but not deterministic.

Evidence level: `gaian_observed` with `peer_reviewed` partial support. Confidence: `medium`.
